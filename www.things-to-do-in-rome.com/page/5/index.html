Piazza della Rotonda

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The piazza in front of the Pantheon is called Piazza della Rotonda. In the middle you can see a fountain, which was made by Giacomo della Porta in 1575. The obelisk that adorns it is from the 13th century BC, from the time of Ramses II. It was stolen by the Romans from Egypt. Romans really liked obelisks and stole so many from Egypt that there are now more Egyptian obelisks in Rome than there are in Egypt.

The Pantheon

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Pantheon is one of the most impressive monuments of ancient Rome. ‘Whoever visits Rome without visiting the Pantheon, returns like an ass’ is what the Romans say. This is probably the best preserved monument from antiquity. According to legend it is built on the spot where Romulus, founder of Rome, was taken by a cloud during a thunderstorm and forever disappeared. On the frieze you can see the words M.AGRIPPA.L.F.COS. TERTIUM.FECIT which means Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius built this during his third consulate. These letters are not original, but were added later. Thanks to the holes in the frieze historians found out what the original letters said and then replicas were made. For a long time people thought the Pantheon was built in the time of Emperor Augustus. Not a strange thought, considering on the facade it says so.

Villa Borghese

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This park is called the Villa Borghese. It was the first of its kind in Rome. In 1901 it became property of the state. In the park you can find museums, foreign academies, a zoo, an artificial lake and an amphitheatre. It is a pleasant park where a lot of Romans come on Sundays to stroll around with their children.

The most important museum you can find here is the Galleria Borghese, which houses the famous baroque statues of Bernini. It is something really worth visiting, but you have to make a reservation in advance, because the museum is small and a lot of people want to go there. There is no point going there without tickets.

Trevi Fountain

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Trevi Fountain is the most famous fountain of Rome. It was made famous by Italian filmmaker Federico Fellini’s film ‘La Dolce Vita’, in which Anita Ekberg takes a bath in the fountain with Marcello Mastroianni watching. I would advise against jumping in now, as it is continuously watched by policemen who prevent people from reenacting the scene. The best time to visit the fountain is two o’clock in the morning. Then, the tourists are all gone and spotlights illuminate the fountain beautifully. The police are still there though.

The fountain was built halfway through the 18th century. It was paid for with a tax on wine. The story goes that the Pope who instituted this tax couldn’t leave his house without eggs and tomatoes being thrown at him. In the end his investment paid off though. Just look at the number of tourists looking at the fountain (unless you are here at four in the morning). The fountain is located against Palazzo Poli. It was designed by Bernini, but the plan was only executed fifty years after his death by Nicola Salvi. Bernini never saw the fountain. The water comes from the Aqua Virgo, which runs under Via Condotti and ends up here.

The central figure is the god of sea Oceanus. On both sides of Oceanus you can see two Tritons who are taming two horses. One is wild and the other one is quiet. This is a reference to the two shapes the sea can take. The water from the fountain flows from here to many other fountains in the city, like the ones on Piazza Navona.

Maybe you have seen people throwing coins in the fountain. According to legend, if you throw a coin with your right hand over your left shoulder (not the other way around), you will return to Rome. If you throw two coins you will also fall in love with an Italian, but watch out, if you throw three, you will marry one. Every year, about 90.000 euro worth of coins is thrown into the fountain. This money is used to keep the fountain in good shape.

The church in front of the fountain is called the Santi Vicenzi ed Anastasio. In this church the hearts of almost all 17th, 18th and 19th century Popes are being kept. This macabre tradition was discontinued by Pope Pius X in the beginning of the 20th century.

Santa Maria del Popolo

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Santa Maria del Popolo is an Augustinian church on Piazza del Popolo. It is very small, but contains work by world-famous artists, like Bernini and Caravaggio. The facade was redone in Baroque style by Bernini in 1660.

Piazza del Popolo was the first thing people would see of Rome as they entered the City. The northern gate was here and most visitors and pilgrims to the city entered here. The gate still stands, but the city now expands much further north.

The House of the Knights of Malta

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

At number 68 in the Via Condotti you can see the house of the smallest sovereign state in the world. It is the house of the Knights of Malta. The order was founded in 1113 to care for the sick and wounded in the holy land. The grandmaster, who resides here, is treated like a head of state in Catholic countries. If you want to join you have to prove that your family is at least two centuries part of the nobility and that sixteen of your forefathers were of noble birth.

Via Condotti

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Via Condotti is probably the most expensive street in Rome. All the Italian and International fashion houses keep shop here. If you want to spot an Italian movie star or reality show contestant, this is the place. It is a great place for window shopping (or real shopping if you have the buying power). Even if you don’t have the cash to buy a Gucci bag or Valentino suit, I can recommend you visit one of the shops. They all look beautiful from the inside. The name of the street comes from the aqueduct which runs below it, the Aqua Virgo.

The Column of the Immaculate Conception

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Further down on Piazza di Spagna you can see an enormous column. It was found in 1777 close to here and put up in 1856 to commemorate the proclamation of the dogma of the Immaculate Conception. This is why there is a statue of Saint Mary on top. Close to is you see the Spanish flag hanging from a building. This is the Spanish embassy to the Vatican and the reason the Piazza is called Piazza di Spagna.

The Obelisk on top of the Spanish Steps

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Obelisk on top of the Spanish Steps in front of the church is a Roman imitation of an Egyptian obelisk. We know this because the hieroglyphs are copied badly from the obelisk that stands on Piazza del Popolo. The Roman sculptor put some of the signs upside down. Originally obelisks were symbols of the rays of the sun. The ancient Egyptians worshipped the sun. Even though the obelisks are pagan symbols, the Popes kept them. You will always see a cross on top of the obelisk though, representing the prevalence of Christianity over Paganism.

The Spanish Steps

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Spanish Steps are by far the most famous steps in Rome and maybe the world. The steps were built in 1726. It is strange that the steps are called Spanish steps, because they were financed by a French ambassador. He wanted to provide a nice walkway to the French Church on top of the hill, the Trinità dei Monti. Before, there were only a few small paths up. In the end they became known as the Spanish steps, because the Piazza below is called Piazza di Spagna. This is because the Spanish Embassy to the Holy See is located on the Piazza. You can see the Spanish flag hanging from a big palazzo close to the column.

The steps consist of ten times twelve steps and have three terraces, which is a reference to the trinity, after which the church on top is named. At the top there is a very nice view of Piazza di Spagna and also the rest of Rome. The dome of Saint Peter’s can be seen. Every spring, many flowers are put on the steps, which make them look even more amazing.

Once, there was a model market on the steps. Painters and sculptors would come here to pick their models. During the touristic high season the steps will be filled with people. Piazza di Spagna can get extremely busy. I always recommend people to do a very very late night tour to see these kinds of monuments without the masses of people. If you pass here at 2 AM on a warm summer night, you will have a really different experience than at 4PM when you can’t see the steps because of the masses of people. The same goes for Trevi Fountain. Go at 2AM, really, you’ll thank me for it (especially if you have gone during the day in the high season).

Ponte Sisto

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Ponte Sisto was built in 1475 during the papacy of Sixtus IV, the same Pope who gave his name to the Sistine Chapel. During the holy year of 1450 the Ponte Sant’Angelo had not been able to accommodate all the pilgrims so a new bridge was made. Now it is a nice pedestrian bridge leading to the Trastevere neighborhood. Trastevere is a good place to find a restaurant or bar. It is beautiful during the day, but really comes to live at night. Friday and Saturday nights the streets are filled with both tourists and Italians.

Piazza and Palazzo Farnese

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Close to Campo di Fiore is Piazza Farnese. It is named after the Palazzo Farnese, which now houses the French Embassy. The palazzo was built using stones from the Colosseum. Maybe you noticed when you were looking at the Colosseum that half of the outer ring is missing. This is where the stones ended up. The architect of Palazzo Farnese got a permit to take as many stones as he could from the Colosseum in 24 hours, so he hired as many people as he could and that resulted in a big part of the Colosseum being destroyed. The palace was built for Alessandro Farnese, who became Pope Paul III. He had been made a cardinal when he was very young, because his sister Giulia had become the mistress of Pope Alexander VI. Michelangelo also worked on the palace and inside there are also famous frescos by Annibale Carraci, which you can only sometimes visit.

On the square you can see two fountains. Ancient bathtubs were taken from the baths of Caracalla to be used for the fountains.

Campo de’ Fiori

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This square is called Campo de’ Fiori, the field of flowers. Until 1431 this was a field where flowers grew. It was also the place where public executions were held. The names of the small streets surrounding Campo still refer to this. Vicolo de Venti refers to the 20 (venti) heretics that were thrown in hot oil here. Via della Corda means Street of the Rope which refers to the Gallows.

The statue in the middle of the Square represents Giordano Bruno, a philosopher and Dominican monk who was put to death by the Catholic Church for his beliefs. He claimed the earth was not the center of the universe and that stars are suns that are spread out throughout the galaxy. The statue was put here in 1887, when the Popes had stopped ruling all of Rome. In the medallions we see portraits of eight other heretics that had to die for their beliefs.

Vanozza Catanei used to run a few inns on campo. She was the lover of Rodrigo Borgia who became Pope Alexander VI. She had four children by the Pope, of which Lucrezia and Cesare Borgia are the most famous.

Campo is one of the centers of Roman nightlife. During the day this is a market, but in the evening the square is filled with a mix of young Italians and tourists who hang outside or in the bars. It is very nice to spend an evening here. It is more authentic than Piazza Navona, which is touristy and expensive.

Santa Maria dell’Anima

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria dell’Anima is the German church. You can find it just behind Piazza Navona. Around this church the German pilgrims used to stay when they came to Rome. In the church is also the grave of the last non-Italian Pope before John Paul II. He was Hadrian VI and he was Dutch. He was made Pope because of the influence of his employer and student, Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor. His pontificate was not a success. He was way too strict for the Romans to handle. They were used to Popes like Alexander VI and Julius II, who had children and were more like renaissance princes. Everybody was very relieved when Hadrian died very soon. Signs of gratitude were put at the door of the Popes physician, who had not been able (or wanting) to save him.

Palazzo Pamphili

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Next to the Sant’Agnese church is Palazzo Pamphili, which now is the Brazilian embassy, but used to be the home of the powerful Pamphili family, the family of Pope Innocentius X. Nowadays this Pope is most famous for the portrait the Spanish artist Diego Velazquez did of him. This painting can be found in the museum Galleria Doria Pamphili, on Via del Corso. Francis Bacon was inspired by this painting for his series of paintings of Screaming Popes.

Sant’Agnese

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Sant’Agnese is a beautiful church on Piazza Navona. Bernini’s rival Borromini worked on it. If you have the chance, have quick look inside because the architecture is beautiful. In the chapel to the left of the choir the head of Saint Agnes is kept. It is called the Sacra Testa. Above the entrance on the inside is the grave of Pope Innocent X.

Fontana del Moro

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Fontana del Moro from 1576 was carved by Giacomo della Porta. You can see a moor, wrestling with a dolphin, surrounded by four blowing sea gods. The figure of the Moor was added in 1654 by Bernini. The Moor is a portrait of the ambassador of the Congo who came to Rome at that time.

Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The fountain in the middle is the Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi, designed and executed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini. It is considered to be one of his masterpieces. Pope Innocent X did not like Bernini, because he had been very close with his predecessor, Urban VIII. Therefore, Innocent did not give Bernini any commissions. Bernini was very clever though and participated anonymously in the contest to design the fountain. Of course his design was a lot better than his competitors and Innocent was forced to hire Bernini. He said ‘the only way to avoid employing Bernini is not see his designs’. The fountain is from 1651 and its message is very typical of the time. It represents the Catholic Message which is diffused over the world. You can see four river gods sitting on rocks. They represent the four continents (the fifth was not yet discovered).
