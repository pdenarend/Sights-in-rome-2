The Forum of Trajan

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Here you can see the Forum of Trajan. Much of this amazing complex is still standing. Trajan was the first Emperor that was not from Rome. He was born in Spain, but from a family of Roman colonists. He was a promising young general when he was adopted by Emperor Nerva. He was a great strategist and won many wars. He conquered the Dacians, whom we now call the Romanians. He is the reason the Romanians still speak a Romance language and so many Romanians come to Italy to look for work. It is very easy for them to learn Italian.

Trajan also builds a lot of public buildings, like Vespasian and Titus before him. They also did not build a lot of buildings for themselves, because they wanted to consolidate their reign by pleasing the public.

Trajan’s forum was finished in 113. He paid for it with the money he made conquering the Dacians. The territory of the Dacians was filled with gold mines. He wanted to build the most impressive of Imperial Forums. His architect was the Apollodorus of Damascus, the most famous architect of that time. The most famous monument on his Forum is still standing. It is Trajan’s Column. On the column you can see the story of Trajan’s conquest of the Dacians in a spiral bas relief. Once the column was painted in bright colors, but now it is just white. It is almost 40 meters high (131 feet). The bas relief tells the story of Trajan’s victory. It is a very interesting source for historians. You can see the Romans build bridges, set up camp and engaging in battle, all in a very realistic style. There are about 2500 figures visible and they are all of high artistic quality. Inside there are stairs which lead to the top.

On top there is now a statue of Saint Peter, which was put there to show Christianity goes above paganism. Originally there was a statue of Trajan there. After Trajan died, his ashes were held in a golden urn at the base of the column. According to a Roman legend, Trajan made it to heaven in the end, although he was never Christian. When Pope Gregory I the great visited the forum in the 6th century he looked at the column and saw a picture of Trajan comforting a woman who had lost her son. Gregory was deeply touched that he prayed for the soul of the deceased Emperor. His prayers were answered because he managed to bring the ashes temporarily back to life. He then baptized Trajan who could then enter heaven. The column is probably still standing because it was used as a bell tower for a medieval church, which now has long disappeared.

Behind the column you can see the actual forum. It was an enormous market and is still standing. It was basically a shopping mall. There were 150 shops on 6 floors. It is made out of brick, which had just been discovered. Downstairs probably dry foods like sugar and flour were sold and you could go upstairs to buy oil and wine. The medieval tower behind the forum is from the 13th century and is called the Torre delle Milizie. Soldiers were based here.

The Forums of Nerva and Augustus

April 9, 2015April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Across the Via dei Fori Imperiali some parts of the Forum of Nerva can be seen. Once, a nice temple of Minerva stood here. You can see all the forums are below the current level of the street. This is because during the last two millennia the city has become higher. You could call this due to the dust of ages. During the middle ages this area was not really used anymore. Cows were held here and slowly, thanks to dust and manure, the level of the ground rose considerably, until archeologist started digging in the 19th century.

Next to the forum of Nerva you can see what is left of the Forum of Augustus. He also built a temple in honor of the god of war Mars here when he won a battle against the murderers of Caesar, his adoptive father. Only three of the 24 columns are left.

The Forum of Vespasian

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The forum of Vespasian is part of the imperial fora that Emperors build, because the forum Romanum, the main market and square of Rome became too small. The remains of these fora can be found under the main Via dei Fori Imperiali, which was constructed under Mussolini. In the map above you can see this main road going across with a map of the old Imperial Fora over it. Big parts of the Imperial fora are buried under the street, but because the street is one of the main traffic arteries of Rome, it will stay under ground. Recently there has been a move to pedestrianize the Via dei Fori Imperiali. The idea is to make the biggest archeological park of Europe, including the Colosseum, Palatine Hill, the Forum Romanum and the Imperial Fora. So far only the traffic in the street has been reduced. Taxis and busses are still allowed to pass.

The Forum of Vespasian is almost completely gone. You still can visit the church of the Saints Cosma and Damiano, which was built inside what probably once was the library of the Forum.

Palatine Hill

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Behind the Arch you can see Palatine hill. This is one of the seven hills on which Rome was built and it was here that everything started. According to legend this was the place Romulus and Remus were nursed by the wolf. Romulus would go on and become Rome’s first king, after murdering his brother. Afterwards this became the place where the Emperors and noble families had their houses. This tradition was started by the first Emperor, Augustus, who built a relatively simple house on the hill, to associate him with the founder of Rome, Romulus. Later Emperors built much bigger palaces on the hill. The English word palace comes from the word Palatinus. The hill is 50 meters high and archaeological evidence shows that the first settlement in this area was in fact on this hill, around 1000 BC.

The last Emperor of the Flavian family was Domitian, the brother of the well-loved Titus. He became crazy with power. He built an enormous palace on palatine hill overlooking the Forum and proclaimed himself a god during his lifetime. He was constantly afraid to be murdered, so he had the walls of his palace made out of polished marble, which had an effect like a mirror. This way he could always see if someone would sneak up behind him to kill him. In the end he was murdered in his bathroom, because there was no polished marble there.

Later Emperors expanded Domitian’s palace. It used to extend across Palatine hill and overlooked the Circus Maximus. The building of the part that is still visibly overlooking the circus was built during the reign of Septimus Severus at the beginning of the 3rd century.

Next to the palace of Severus is the so-called Hippodrome of Domitian. It looks like a Roman circus but is in fact too small to hold chariot races. It was probably a stadium for athletic competitions, like sprinting. One of the first martyrs, Saint Sebastian, is said to be clubbed to death on palatine hill, after first being pierced by arrows.

The Meta Sudans was the fountain where the victorious gladiators would wash the blood and sand from their bodies. The base of the Meta Sudans can be seen in the above picture in the brown circular region just in front of the arch. The Arch of Constantine

April 9, 2015April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Next to the Colosseum you can see the Arch of Constantine. It was built in honor of Emperor Constantine. He was one the most important figures in western history. He was the first Emperor that became Christian. Historians are not quite sure if he ever was baptized. Most people believe he was baptized just before dying, because the Emperor thought it was impossible to be a good Christian and a good Emperor at the same time. This is the last monument built in classical Rome, because Emperor Constantine soon moved the capital to a new city on the Bosporus, which came to be known as Constantinople, now Istanbul.

Constantine became Christian because the day before an important battle he had a dream in which he saw a cross. An angel then told him ‘In hoc signo vinces’ or in this sign you will conquer. The next day Constantine ordered all his soldiers to paint crosses on their shield and of course he won the battle and became the sole Emperor. Afterwards he started sponsoring Christianity. He ordered the building of Saint Peter’s Basilica and Saint John of Lateran. He also presided over Church councils, like the one in Milan, in which Christianity was legalized. Constantine’s influence was enormous. Before Constantine about 5% of the population of the empire was Christian, after Constantine about 60 to 70% of the population were Christian.

This arch was made by using parts of older arches, celebrating other Emperors. Only the carvings just above the arches have something to do with Constantine. Elsewhere we see for example hunting scenes from the time of Hadrian. The inscription says ‘The senate and the people of Rome devote this arch to Constantine, because he liberated the state from tyranny and infighting thanks to his godly inspiration and greatness of his mind.

The Colosseum

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

As long as the Colosseum exists Rome will exist. When the Colosseum will be destroyed, Rome will be destroyed and when Rome will be destroyed, the world will be destroyed’. These famous words were spoken by an English monk in the 8th century AD. It was also him, Beda Venerabilis, who was the first one to call this building Colosseum. The Romans never knew this building as the Colosseum; they called it something completely different. They knew it as the Flavian Amphitheatre.

An Amphitheatre is basically a building which consists of two theatres put together. It was called Flavian after the name of the ruling family that constructed it. It was Emperor Vespasian, the first of the Flavian Emperors, who had it built. He was the Emperor that managed to take power in the civil war that followed Nero’s death. He had made his fame and fortune in Judea, modern Israel where he had stopped an uprising. When Vespasian took power he had to consolidate his reign. Nero, of course, had been crazy, but he was part of the first imperial family and could trace his ancestry back to the first Emperor Augustus. Vespasian was not even from a very noble family. So to get the people to like him he constructed the Colosseum, right on top of Nero’s enormous villa, the Domus Aurea.

Piazza della Repubblica

April 8, 2015April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Piazza della Repubblica was once part of the huge baths of Diocletian. The half circular shape of the piazza still reflects this. This part used to be the entrance hall to the baths. Parts of the actual baths are still standing on one side of the piazza. Bathing was very important for the ancient Romans and was an important part of everybody’s life. Everybody went to the baths. Men, women, slaves and masters, poor people and very rich. Of course everybody had different hours on which they were allowed to go to the baths. Personal hygiene was very important. The Romans believed in the idea of a healthy mind in a healthy body. Rome had a lot of very imposing baths, because all of the one million people that lived in ancient Rome went to bathe. It was also a place to do business meetings or to meet up with friends. Besides bathing, you could also get a massage, go to the gym or have a relaxing walk in the gardens. Of course, also stories are known of theft, murder and orgies that took place in the baths. In Pompeii archeologists found this inscription: ‘Bathing, wine and sex wreck our body, but what is life without bathing, wine and sex’.

Via Veneto

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Via Veneto used to be the most glamorous street in Rome. It was the center of the Dolce Vita in the 60s and 70s. Still, a lot of the most expensive and famous hotels and bars are located on this street. It was made famous by filmmaker Federico Fellini, who shot parts of ‘La dolce vita’ here. Thanks to the success of the movie, the street became soon filled with tourists, who made the international movie stars and Roman elites leave.

The American embassy is also located in an enormous palazzo on this street. Queen Margarita, of pizza fame, used to live here.

Cripta dei Capuccini

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

In this church, one of the most macabre sights of Rome can be seen. The church is called Santa Maria della Concezione, but the adjacent crypt is better known. It is the Cripta dei Cappuccini. The church was commissioned by Antonio Barberini, twin brother of Pope Urban VIII. The most famous artwork in the church is the painting of Archangel Michael defeating the devil, by Guido Reni. The angel was so beautiful that people thought Guido had gone to heaven to paint the angel, also because the painter had not been seen for quite some time. Guido said: ‘The beauty of the angel you cannot find on earth. I have not seen him and he springs from my fantasy only, but the devil I have seen many times, so I know how he looks.

There is also a copy of a painting by Caravaggio, representing Saint Francis. The original hangs in the room of the prior and cannot be visited.

Next to the church is the macabre crypt. The capuchin monks were moved to this convent by Urban VIII. They agreed, but asked the Pope if they could take their deceased brothers with them. The Pope then gave them earth from Jerusalem for their crypt. The bones of their brothers were then dug up by the light of torches and were taken to this church. The bones were then used to decorate the crypt. The second crypt is filled with bones from soldiers who volunteered to protect the Pope when Italian troops were trying to conquer Rome in the Risorgimento. They died in 1870 and were buried here by the capuchin monks. On the right side you can see the skeleton of Prince Matteo Orsini, who according to his wishes was buried standing up, wearing his habit. In the 5th and last crypt, visitors are reminded that ‘What you are, we were, and what we are, you shall become’.

Piazza Barberini

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

On Piazza Barberini you can see the famous Fontana del Tritone, sculpted by Gian Lorenzo Bernini in 1640. Its brassy color is just a result of a discoloring of the white marble. Triton sits on a shell and blows water from a horn. The shell is carried by a dolphin’s tail.

Around the corner you can find Palazzo Barberini, which was built in 1625 by Carlo Maderno, the architect of the facade of Saint Peter’s. It was built for the Barberini Pope Urban VIII who also commissioned the fountain on the piazza. His coat-of-arm consisted of three bees on a shield. This is why you see this everywhere in this building and also on the fountain. Now the Palazzo houses the Galleria Nazionale D’Arte Antica, which is a very nice museum with some interesting paintings by artists like Caravaggio. It is one of the lesser known museums in Rome, but their collection of paintings is amazing.

Trastevere

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Trastevere used to be a popular neighborhood, where typical roman families used to live. This aspect of the neighborhood has disappeared a little. Now many tourists, but also Romans from all over the city come here to have dinner or to drink. It is a fun area and very beautiful. Once, in Roman times, this area was populated by slaves, seamen, Syrians and Jews and lay outside the city walls. This was the place where foreign traders lived. The name means across the Tevere, which is the Italian name for the Tiber. In the picture you can see the Piazza of Santa Maria in Trastevere, the social center of the neighborhood.

Isola Tiberina

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Between Trastevere and the ghetto lies the Tiber Island, the isola Tiberina. In Roman times, the Temple of Asclepius was here. He was the god of medicine and so talented he could raise people from the dead. His daughters were called Hygieia and Panacea. If you were sick you would go to the temple of Asclepius. You would sleep there, and if you saw a snake in your dream you would have been healed.

Later a hospital for people suffering from the plague was here. This way the patients could be put in quarantine. Even nowadays there is a hospital on the island. It a tradition that is older than 2000 years. Quite amazing!

According to legend, the island was created when the Romans threw the grain supply of the last King of Rome, Tarquinius Superbus, in the river when they deposed him and founded the republic. Earth was thrown on top of the grain and the many seeds created the island. In reality the island is of volcanic origin.

To the south of the island stands the Ponte Rotto, the broken bridge. It was built in 142 BC and it was the first stone bridge that crossed the Tiber. It was also the first arched bridge in the world. It was destroyed by a flood somewhere in the 16th century but the middle part still stands in the water.

The Ghetto

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The area between Capitoline Hill and the river used to be the Jewish Ghetto. From the 2nd century onward Jewish people lived in Rome. They used to live across the river in Trastevere. There were 13 synagogues in ancient Rome and they were allowed to exercise their faith freely. This situation changed radically in the middle ages. Jewish men had to wear a yellow hat and women a yellow ribbon. Many more Jews came to Rome after their expulsion from the Kingdom of Spain in 1492. Then, on the 26th of July by order of Pope Paul IV the Jews were locked up in a Ghetto. They were not allowed to leave at night and the ghetto could be closed off by big gates. They were just allowed to trade in second hand clothing, wool, furs and iron scraps. The Jews were only freed from the Ghetto in 1798 when the troops of Napoleon conquered Rome and abducted the Pope to Paris. The walls were only torn down in 1870.

Some inhabitants of the Ghetto were deported in the Second World War. On 16 October 1943, the SS went around the area around the Porticus of Octavia and captured about 1000 Jewish people. They were deported to Auschwitz. Only 17 managed to survive. There is a sign about this on the piazza in front of the Porticus of Octavia, which is called Largo 16 ottobre 1943.

In the main street of the Ghetto stands this Roman doorway. It is called the Porticus of Octavia. It was a big square, surrounded by 300 columns. There also was a library here. Now, just the entrance gate is left. This also was built by Emperor August, in honor of his sister Octavia, the mother of Marcellus. Of Emperor Augustus it was said that he found Rome in brick and left her in marble. Augustus ruled for a very long time. It was a prosperous and peaceful time for the empire. His reign is sometimes called the Pax Augusta, the peace of Augustus.

This very odd looking building once was the Theatre of Marcellus. You can clearly see that in Roman times it was a Theatre, but then in the middle ages a powerful aristocratic family, the Orsini, made it into a fort and palace.

Emperor Augustus built the theatre for his nephew Marcellus, who was supposed to succeed him. Unfortunately, Marcellus died at a very young age.

20.000 people could be seated here. It is older than the Colosseum. At the opening of the theatre, some parts of the seating collapsed and people wanted to leave, but Augustus himself sat down at the most dangerous spot to keep people from leaving. This was a real theatre and not an arena, so no gladiator fights where held here, but still, in some plays one actor would really die. Convicted criminals were used for this. Next to the theatre are some remains of a very old temple of Apollo.

The Synagogue was built in 1904. The building of the synagogue was meant to show the Jews that the new Italian state would do a better job in looking out for them than the Papacy ever did. The synagogue has a big dome, a challenge to Saint Peter’s. The Pope became very angry and laid a ban on the new Italian state.

Now about 15.000 Jews live in Rome. Most of them live outside the ghetto these days.

Pope John Paul II was the first Pope to visit the synagogue in 1986. It was also visited later by Pope Benedict XVI.

Piazza del Campidoglio on Capitoline Hill

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This hill is one of the seven on which Rome was originally built. It is called Capitoline hill and is the smallest of the seven. The buildings you see when you stand on piazza Campidoglio were built in the renaissance, but in Roman times this hill was one of the most important places in Rome, together with Palatine hill across the forum, where the Emperors lived. On Capitoline hill the most important temple of the Roman Empire stood. It was the enormous Temple of Jupiter Optimus Maximus, with golden roof. The foundations of the temple can still be seen inside the Capitoline Museums. Just looking at the foundations will make you realize how big the temple really was. Besides the Temple there were also smaller sanctuaries and altars. On the south side of the hill was a steep cliff from which traitors were thrown to their deaths. On the spot where now the Spartan church Santa Maria in Aracoeli stands, the temple of Juno Moneta once stood. In this temple the Roman mint was located. All the Roman coins were produced here and the English word Mint and money comes from the name of this temple: Juno Moneta.

After the fall of the Roman Empire, this area was left to fall apart. The temples were torn down to build new buildings and it became an insignificant hill until the renaissance, when Pope Paul III commissioned Michelangelo to redesign the hill. His design is very beautiful as you can see when you stand on Piazza del Campidoglio on top of the hill. He completely changed the focus of the hill. He made a big staircase, the cordonata, on what used to be the backside of the hill. This is because the center of Rome had changed from the Forum to the area around Piazza Navona. He designed the piazza using a strictly symmetrical floor plan.

Around the square you can see three public buildings. On the east side, built on top of the remains of the Tabularium, the archives of ancient Rome, stands the Palazzo Senatorio, which nowadays is the City Hall of Rome. In the middle, below the terrace, he put an antique statue, which represents Rome, which is flanked by two statues of River Gods, the Nile and the Tiber. If you come here on Sunday, you often see people getting married.

The Concordata is the access road to the square. It has very low steps, in order to allow horses to ascend as well. At the bottom of the stairs you can see two lions, who were found in the Campus Martius, but who are from Egypt. At the top of the Concordata you can see two statues on both sides holding horses. They wear strange hats. This is because they are Castor and Pollux, sons of Leda and Jupiter. Jupiter disguised himself as a swan when he seduced Leda, so Castor and Pollux were born out of two eggs. This is why they wear an eggshell on their heads. When one of them died, Jupiter made both of them immortal and put them in the sky as the sign Gemini.

Halfway down the stairs you can see the small statue of Cola di Rienzo, who was a Medieval populist who wanted to restore the grandeur of ancient Rome. Until 1960 there were two cages here, one with a live wolf and one with an eagle, the two symbols of Rome. Animal rights activists managed to convince the city to stop this tradition.

On the south side lies Palazzo dei Conservatori and on the north side Palazzo Nuovo. The Palazzo dei Conservatori already existed, but Michelangelo gave it a new renaissance facade, which is identical to the one of Palazzo Nuovo on the other side of the square. These two palaces harbor the Capitoline Museums. They are connected to each other under the earth by a tunnel which leads through the old Roman Tabularium, under the City Hall. It was the first publicly accessible museum in the world. Clement XII opened it in 1734 for the general public.

If you have the time, go visit the museums. The museums have a mixed collection. You see many world famous roman statues, like the original equestrian statue of Marcus Aurelius. Also, you can see the archeological excavations of the foundations of the temple of Jupiter. I sometimes go there with tour groups if it rains. It makes for a nice excursion, especially if the weather outside is not the best.

In the middle of the square stands the equestrian statue of Marcus Aurelius. It is a copy of an original which now stands inside the Capitoline Museums. The original was put here by Michelangelo and stood here for many centuries, until it was moved to protect it. The original is also made of bronze and it is just luck that it still exists. Many bronze statues were melted down, but people thought this statue represented Emperor Constantine, the first Christian Emperor, so it was saved. Later it turned out to represent Marcus Aurelius.

The base of the statue is the only sculpture by Michelangelo that is still standing outside. Now, if you take an Italian fifty euro cent coin, you will see this statue, together with the design on the floor. These geometrical shapes were also designed by Michelangelo, but only recently executed.

Piazza Venezia

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Piazza Venezia is the heart of Rome. It is named after the Palazzo that stands next to it, which was built in 1455 by a cardinal, Pietro Barbo, from Venice. He later became Pope Paul II. Most of its fame derives from the fact that Mussolini had his offices here and often held speeches from the balcony. He always kept the lights on, even when he was not there, to show he was working hard for the Italian people.

Of course the most impressive building here is the Monument for Victor Emanuel II, the first king of Italy. Italy only exists as a country since 1870, before this the peninsula was divided into a lot of small states. When Italy became united it was felt that an appropriate monument should be erected. This monument was meant as a counterpart to Saint Peter’s. The Italians felt that their capital should not only have an enormous monument to the church, but also a monument to their new fatherland. Unfortunately, the design did not please the Romans. They call it the wedding cake or the typewriter. The monument was finished in 1911 and since 1921 the grave of the Unknown Soldier is also here. The fountain to the left represents the Tyrrhenian Sea and the one on the right the Adriatic Sea. On the top are the triumphal chariots of unity and freedom, and of course, in the middle, you can see Victor Emanuel on his horse.

You can go up the stairs and in the building is a free museum about the Risorgimento, the unification of Italy. On the back of the building you can find an elevator. If you pay the ticket it will take you to the top of the building where you have an amazing view of the city.

Piazza Mattei

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Piazza Mattei is a cute little square, famous for the Fontana delle Tartarughe, the turtle fountain. It was made in 1584 by Giacomo della Porta. The turtles were added by Bernini. According to legend, the fountain was constructed in one night on the orders of a young aristocrat, who wanted to proof to the dad of the girl he loved that, in spite of rumors heard around town, he was financially solvent.

This piazza lied on the border of the free Rome and the Jewish Ghetto, just behind here.

Teatro Argentino

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

On Largo Torre Argentina you can see a theatre. It is the most important theatre of Rome. Rossini premiered his Barbiere di Seviglia here. It was a complete disaster. Fans of another composer came to heckle Rossini, who at one point turned around and applauded the spectators. He then had to flee the theatre. The next day the show went according to plan and the Barbiere di Sevigla has been a classic ever since.

Largo di Torre Argentina

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This area is called Largo di Torre Argentina. Here you can see the remains of four temples from republican times. They were found in 1926. We don’t know to which gods they were dedicated, so we call the temples A, B, C and D. The temples were excavated in the time of Mussolini. Some people even accused him of just putting some rubble together just to show he had found another remnant of ancient Rome.

Maybe you have seen the many cats strolling around the temples. This is because on one side there is a cat sanctuary. Abandoned cats are being taken care of. You can even go downstairs and have a look around, but watch out, it really smells of cats. The volunteers sell some fun souvenirs as well.

Somewhere around the square the murder of Julius Caesar took place. The senate held meetings here while the senate building on the forum was being restored. In 44 BC Caesar was murdered by his fellow senators, who felt his reforms deprived them of power. More than 60 men conspired against him and he was stabbed to death by 23 stabs as he put his toga over his head.

San Luigi dei Francesi

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The San Luigi dei Francesi is the national church of France in Rome. It was designed by Giacomo della Porta and executed by Domenico Fontana between 1518 and 1589. The facade has several statues with famous people from the history of France, like Charlemagne, Saint Louis, Saint Clothilde and Saint Jeanne of Valois.

Inside is one of the masterpieces of Domenichino, a famous baroque painter. On the ceiling he painted the histories of Saint Cecilia. Domenichino was for a period considered one of the best painters of all time, but his fame is somewhat diminished now.

Sant’Ignazio

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you pass by the Sant’Ignazio, you should definitely go in, even if it is just for a second. But before you do so, have a look at the Piazza Sant’Ignazio in front of it. It is a very nice example of baroque architecture. Look at how playful the buildings are built. This is not a geometric design of the renaissance anymore. Everything is rounded. Then, when you go inside, you will be amazed by the ceiling of the church.

It is an amazing example of perspective painting. If you stand on the marker in the middle of the nave of the church and look up, the perspective in the ceiling fresco is exactly right and the ceiling looks much higher than it actually is. But if you move to other parts of the church, the illusion is broken. You can try it by walking all the way to the altar and look back to the ceiling of the nave. It is an amazing fresco and it was painted by a Jesuit monk, called Andrea Pozzo, who was also a mathematician. In it, he depicts the triumph of Saint Ignatius, the founder of the Jesuit order. Right in the middle of the fresco you can see Jesus, hanging on to his cross. From Jesus, a ray of light shoots towards Ignatius, who is sitting on a cloud. The light is then reflected by Ignatius and illuminates the four continents. These continents are reflected by the four pillars in the corners of the fresco. Their names are written on the pillars as well: Africa, Europe, Asia and America. Australia had not yet been discovered. The continents are also recognizable by symbols. A Native American can be seen around the American pillar and a black queen around the African.

Then there is also the dome. The dome is not real, but also painted. The monks of the adjacent monastery did not want the big dome blocking the light in their library, so they asked the Jesuits to not build a dome. They did not build a dome, but instead asked Andrea Pozzo to paint one. It is a gigantic trompe-l’oeil.

The church itself is a typical example of Roman baroque style. It is, of course, a Jesuit church and the baroque was a style the Jesuits often liked to use in their buildings.

Santa Maria Sopra Minerva

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This church, adjacent to the Pantheon, is the only Gothic church in Rome. It is called Santa Maria Sopra Minerva. Sopra means above, so the church was built on top of a temple dedicated to Minerva. Churches were often built on top of temples. After Christianity replaced paganism, the temples were no longer needed and were considered blasphemous. So they were torn down and with the building material recovered churches were built.

The facade is simple and dates from the renaissance, but the interior of the church is gothic. The gothic style was never very popular in Italy and at the time of the style’s popularity the Popes lived in Avignon in France, so not much was built in Rome at that time. The church belongs to the Dominican friars. On the right side on the facade markers can be seen which show how high the water would rise sometimes. This area was prone to flooding until the banks of the Tiber were strengthened in the 19th century.
