The Temple of Vespasian and Titus

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Of the Temple of Vespasian and Titus only three Corinthian columns are left. Both Emperors were deified after their death. They were from the Flavian family and were responsible for the building of the Colosseum. It was a short-lived dynasty, because after Titus died his brother Domitian became Emperor and soon turned crazy with power. He was murdered and then the era of the adoptive Emperors started, with great Emperors like Trajan, Hadrian and Marcus Aurelius.

Via Sacra

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Via Sacra was the main road of the forum. Military and religious processions were held here who all ended at the temple of Jupiter on Capitoline hill. Many army leader and Emperor were welcomed here. If a victory was obtained someone could get a triumph. He was then allowed to hold a procession to show the spoils of war. Many barbarian kings were also shown to the people in chains or cages on this road.

Tabularium

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This building once was the government archives. It was called the Tabularium. It was built on the slope of Capitoline hill. Big parts can still be visited, but from within the Capitoline museums. As you can see the lower part of the building is made out of ancient stones, but the upper part is more modern. This is because the modern city hall was built on top of the Tabularium in the renaissance.

The Temple of Saturn

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Higher up on the Forum Romanum you can see the Temple of Saturn. It is originally from the 5th century BC. This temple housed the treasury of the state. Also, weapons were kept here in case the Gauls would show up. Saturn was the father of Jupiter. He ruled as the highest god before him. He once heard a prophecy that one of his sons would one day take his place, so every time he fathered a son he would immediately eat him. Jupiter’s mother was smart. Instead of giving Saturn his son to eat, she wrapped a stone in a cloth and Saturn ate the stone. He never found out, until Jupiter grew up and replaced him as Highest God.

Rostrum

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

In the middle, in front of the actual forum, you can see the Rostrum. This used to be a stage on which important speeches were held. Marc Anthony held his famous speech here after Julius Caesar had been killed. What he exactly said is unknown to us, but many people know Shakespeare’s rendition of the speech which starts with the famous words: Friends, Romans, Countrymen. Can you see the vertical lines on the front wall of the Rostra? Augustus had attached prows of the ships he had destroyed at the battle of Actium.

Cicero held many speeches here, including 14 very hostile speeches against Marc Anthony. He later took revenge by ordering the killing of Cicero and displaying his hands and head on the stage. It is said that more people came to see his head than had ever come to see his speeches.

Curia

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This is the spot where the senate held its sessions. It once started with 100 senators, but in the time of Caesar there were 900. Emperor Augustus reduced their number to 600. The building burned down in 52 BC in the time of Julius Caesar. He paid for the restoration, but the senate was temporarily moved across town. So Caesar was not murdered on this spot, because at that time the building was being reconstructed. The present building is another reconstruction from the time of Diocletian in the 3rd century. Why do you think it is still standing? This is, as almost always with ancient constructions, because it was transformed into a church. Mussolini then changed it back to how it was before.

The Triumphal Arch of Septimus Severus

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The triumphal arch of Septimus Severus was built in 203 AD in remembrance of his 10 years as Emperor and because of his victory against the Parthians. It was also dedicated to his sons Caracalla and Geta, but when Caracalla became Emperor he had his brother murdered and all references to his brother were taken down from the arch. You can see that there is a hole somewhere in the fourth line of the inscription. This is where Geta’s name could be read. In the middle ages the arch stood half in the earth and a barber shop was run from under there. Caracalla’s real name was Caesar Marcus Aurelius Antoninus Augustus Pius Felix but his nickname was Caracalla, which was the name of the Gallic Cape he always wore and made fashionable.

The Temple of Castor and Pollux

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

In the middle of the Forum Romanum you can see the Temple of Castor and Pollux. Only three Corinthian columns are still standing. It was built in 484 BC. Castor and Pollux were twins. Their father was Jupiter, who had seduced their mother Leda by taking the shape of a swan. Castor and Pollux were then born out of eggs. They were very important for the Romans. They believed the twins had helped them during an important battle against the Latin people at the lake of Regilius in 486 BC. After the battle 2 strong unknown warriors were seen on this spot, giving water to their horses. Afterwards these warriors disappeared but the Romans believed they had been Castor and Pollux. Then a temple was built on this spot.

The Temple of Antoninus and Faustina

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This Temple is the Temple of Antoninus and Faustina. Antoninus is not a very famous Emperor. He was one of the adoptive Emperors. After Emperor Domitian became crazy and was murdered the senate decided that a new system for appointing Emperors had to be found. So they appointed one of their own, Nerva, Emperor. Nerva was not allowed to let his son be Emperor, but he had to adopt a promising young man as his successor. So Nerva adopted Trajan, who became one of the best Emperors, Trajan adopted Hadrian and Hadrian adopted Antoninus Pius. Antoninus is not very famous even though he was a very good Emperor. This is because during his reign nothing happened. The empire was at peace and a lot of people prospered. Antoninus then adopted Marcus Aurelius, who made his son Commodus Emperor. If you have ever seen the movie Gladiator you know that this did not work out very well.

Basilica Aemilia

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

As you walk down you can see the foundation of the Basilica Aemilia on your right side. It was built in 197 BC. This building was meant to protect people from the rain or the sun. The activities that took place outside on the forum had to be continued on rainy days. it was some sort of trade center. Also, magistrates would hold trials here. That is also where the name comes from. Basilica comes from the Greek word Basileus, which means king. Of course, in ancient times it was the king who would administer justice. It was paid for by the Aemilia family, which was one of the most powerful families at that time.

The Forum Romanum

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Forum Romanum once was the heart of Rome. From the 6th century BC onward, this was the place where the most important public buildings stood. It was the political and social center of first the republic and then the empire. Forum means market in Latin, so it all started as the main market square of the city. Before, this area was a swamp. Archaeologists have discovered that the romans added 5 meters of sand to this area so it could be used as a market. Also, a very large drain was constructed, the cloaca maxima, which transported rain water directly to the Tiber. The forum lies between Palatine hill and Capitoline hill. Of course now not much is left. If you walk around without realizing where you stand it is not a very impressive place. Now, of most buildings only the foundations are left. In the middle ages and the renaissance this area was used like a quarry, like the Colosseum. In fact, if you enter a medieval church in Rome you can often notice how every pillar is different. This is because they were taken from different Roman buildings.

San Clemente

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The San Clemente is one of the most interesting churches in Rome. It consists of three layers. Under the main church from the 12th century you can find another church from the 4th century and under that there is an old roman house with a sanctuary to Mithras. The first church on the middle level was erected in the 4th century and named after the 4th Pope Saint Clement, who used to live on this spot. This church was partly destroyed in the 11th century by a Norman conqueror and then a new church was built on top of the remains of the others.

Clement was banished to the Crimea by the Romans because of his success in converting people to Christianity, but even from there he kept on converting lots of people. He then was given an ultimatum: Or worship the Roman gods or die. He decided to continue converting and was then thrown into the Black sea, tied to an anchor. Afterwards, the sea would open once a year, so people could erect a shrine and worship Clements relics. On one of these occasions a widow lost her only son, but he was found alive and well a year later when the sea opened again.

Santi Quattro Coronati

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santi Quattro Coronati is a very interesting but relatively unknown church. It used to be a fortress and monastery, which protected the Lateran palace, where the Popes used to live before they moved to southern France and then to the Vatican. It was built in the 4th century and rebuilt in the 11th century after the Normans attacked. Now, Augustinian nuns live here. The floor is especially notable, because at least 200 antique marble stones were used to make the pattern.

Scala Sancta

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Next to Saint John in Lateran, on the other side of the street, you can find the Scala Sancta. This building houses the holy steps. The building was built by Domenico Fontana in 1589. According to legend Christ climbed the steps in this building on the Friday of his death, going to Pontius Pilatus’ house. The mother of Emperor Constantine, Saint Helen, would have taken the steps from Pilatus’ house in Jerusalem in 326 AD. This story only starts being told in the 7th century though. Only faithful are allowed to ascend the steps, and only on their knees. The steps are protected by wood and on some places you can see some blood, allegedly from Christ.

The Baptistery of St. John Lateran

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you have time try to go and see the Baptistery. It is a small building just outside St. John Lateran. It is the oldest of Rome and according to legend this is the place Emperor Constantine would have been baptized by Pope Sylvester I. The current building dates from 432. Its octagonal shape would influence the building of all later Baptisteries all over the world. The octagonal shape represents the 8th day of creation, when a man is created again by being baptized. It is built on the baths of the old house of Faustina, the second wife of Constantine. The basin is an old roman bathtub made from green basalt. The basin had to be big, because in this time adults were converted to Christianity and had to be baptized by submerging their whole body in water.

You can see frescoes with scenes from the life of John the Baptist and also scenes from the life of Constantine. The mosaic is from the 5th century. The deer represents the renewal of life. The deer blows water towards snakes in holes in the earth to get them out and trample them, just like god uses water to baptize people and drive out evil. The deer always looks for water to drink which represents humanities need for baptism.

The Obelisk of St. John Lateran

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Egyptian Obelisk which stands on the piazza on the side of St. John Lateran is the oldest and highest in Rome. It is 3500 years old and 31 meters high. It was taken from Thebes to Rome in 357 BC in a boat. It used to stand at the Circus Maximus and was later put here by the Popes.

Santa Maria della Vittoria

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria della Vittoria is a very small church. Its fame derives from the statue which can be found inside. It is one of Bernini’s masterpieces, Teresa in Ecstasy. The church used to be a convent of the Carmelites. This order was reformed by Saint Teresa of Ávila. Teresa is one of three female doctors of the Church. She died in the night of the 4th to the 15th of October 1582. Incidentally, this was the night when a few days were skipped because of the introduction of the Gregorian calendar. She was canonized in 1622 and a few decades later Bernini designed the Cornaro chapel in this church to honor her.

Termini

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The main station in Rome is called Termini. This has nothing to do with the English word terminal. It is Latin for ‘at the bathhouses’, the Terme. Diocletian’s bathhouses were (and still are) very close to here. On this spot a small station was built in 1873, but it was too small, so Mussolini decided a new, imposing station should be built in 1937. The building as we see it now was opened only in 1950. The building’s purpose was to show off the greatness of Fascist Italy. If you look at the sides of the building you can see the design was partly taken from the Colosseum. This is typical fascist style. It is very simple, going back to classical shapes without too many ornaments.

Santa Prassede

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This church is called Santa Maria Prassede, and although it is not well known, it is one of the oldest and most beautiful churches of Rome. The church is dedicated Praxedes, the daughter of a Roman senator, Pudens. Peter stayed in his house for some time and converted him to Christianity, together with her daughters Praxedes and Pudentiana. Praxedes dedicated her life to helping the poor and persecuted. She hid Christians in her house to protect them from Roman persecutions. When the Romans found out where their hiding place was they killed all 23 of them. Praxedes then used a sponge to collect their blood, which she kept in the well of her house. The Church is built on the location of her house.

Santa Maria Maggiore

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria Maggiore is one of the four papal basilicas of Rome and is also Vatican territory. It was built on the top of Esquiline hill, because of a dream of a Pope. In his dream, Pope Liberius was visited by an angel, who told him to build a church on top of the hill where snow would fall the next day. Of course, it was the middle of August, and snow does not normally fall at that time of the year. But the Pope woke up and saw that snow had fallen on top of Esquiline hill. He went over and discovered that the owner of the land had had the same dream. They then decided to build the church. This was in the 4th century. Every august this event is remembered by throwing white flowers in the air on August the 5th. The campanile, the bell tower, is a Romanesque structure from the 14th century. The facade is both baroque and medieval. If you stand in front of the Church, you can see that a newer, baroque facade is built around the older one. You can still see the medieval mosaic on what used to be the facade.

On the square in front of the church stands a Corinthian column, which came from Constantine’s Basilica on the Forum. On top, a bronze statue of Mary was put in 1614.

The Forum of Caesar

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This area is the Forum of Caesar. You can see his statue standing in front of it on the side of the Via dei Fori Imperiali. He paid for this forum with the booty he captured in Gaul. In it was a temple to Venus. Caesar claimed his ancestry back to Venus.
