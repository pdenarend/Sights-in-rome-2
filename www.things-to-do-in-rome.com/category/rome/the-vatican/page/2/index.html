The Cathedra Petri

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Cathedra Petri, at the back of Saint Peter’s was also made by Gian Lorenzo Bernini. To give you an idea of the dimensions, the wingspan of the dove is almost 2 meters or a little over 6 foot. The windows are made of alabaster. This is a stone which you would get if you compressed marble for millions of years. It has an interesting quality. It lets light through, which is why it was used here. It represents the light of the Holy Spirit. Next to the alabaster windows you can see small figures that become bigger the further they get from the window. Bernini wants to say here that all beings originate from the light of God.

Below you can see the seat of Saint Peter. It is being held up by four Church fathers. According to legend pieces of Saint Peter’s chair was used to create this bronze chair, but we now know this chair was probably a gift from an early French King. This chair was already in the old Basilica and had to get a new spot in the new one.

The Statue of St. Andrew

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The last statue under the dome of St. Peter’s is Saint Andrew, Patron Saint of Scotland and Greece. He was Peter’s brother and went to Greece to spread the Christian faith. He cured the Roman Governor’s wife of a serious illness and converted her to Christianity, but it is said that he persuaded her to also deny her husband his marital rights, which is what got him beheaded, not his preaching. The head however is no longer here, a treaty was signed in 1966 with the Greek Orthodox Church and as a token of good will the head was sent back to Greece.

The Statue of St. Longinus

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Around the baldacchino in St. Peter’s are four sculptures in the pillars that support the Dome. Relics of these four Saints are kept inside the pillars. The first we see is Saint Longinus, which was sculpted by Bernini. He is the Roman soldier that put his spear in the side of Christ when he was on the cross. Right when Longinus did this he was struck by light and realized Christ was truly God’s son. He then converted and became a Saint. We see him here just after he realized Christ was truly God’s son. He is struck by light, and of course here the light is also present. It comes from the dome above him. The original spear was donated to the Vatican and it’s kept in above the statue, behind the doors on the balcony.

The Baldacchino

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Baldacchino, the structure which covers the altar, is made of bronze and was designed by Bernini, for Pope Urban VIII from the Barberini family. If you look closely you can see small bees everywhere on the building. This is because the Barberini coat-of-arms consists of three bees. Bernini was only 26 years old at the time and it is an incredible and famous work. When it was built they needed bronze, so the Pope ordered for the bronze to be taken from the roof of the Pantheon. This is why there is a saying in Rome that says “what the barbarians didn’t do, the Barberini did). It weighs 120 tons and it took almost 10 years to build. The baldacchino represents heaven.

The Dome of St. Peter’s

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you look up in the middle of Saint Peter’s, you can see Michelangelo’s dome. It is a beautiful piece of architecture. His idea was to make a dome like the Pantheon’s, but lift it up on pillars, and that is exactly what we see. He himself thought the dome of his native Florence was more beautiful and he made the diameter of his dome half a meter shorter than that of the Pantheon, in honor of the unknown architect of that building. The word around the dome say in Latin ‘Tu es Petrus et super hanc petram aedificabo ecclesiam meam et tibi dabo claves regni caelorum‘: You are Peter and upon this rock I will build My Church and I will give you the keys to heaven’, the famous sentence Jesus said and on which the Popes base their authority as successors of Saint Peter, who of course is considered the first Pope. The dome weighs 56 million kilograms and is 136.5 meter high, which is 447.8 feet. You can climb the dome. The entrance is outside the Church on the right side, looking from the square.

The grave of John Paul II

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

On the right side of Saint Peter’s Basilica you can see something new. The body of Pope John Paul II was transferred here recently after he was blessed. He now is one of the few Popes that are buried inside the Basilica and not in the crypts below.

Close to the grave you find the entrance to the Chapel of the Holy Sacrament

This is the only place in the Basilica where you can pray in silence. Inside you are not allowed to take pictures. The altar inside was designed by Gian Lorenzo Bernini, the famous baroque sculptor.

Also near here is the funerary monument for Pope Gregory XIII. He is the one who commissioned the gallery of the maps inside the Vatican Museums. He founded the Astrology department at the Vatican and is still famous for giving us the Gregorian calendar, the calendar we still use today. Before this they used the Julian calendar, which Julius Caesar had introduced. This calendar was wrong, because every year was 11 minutes too short, so after a lot of years, the calendar was evidently wrong. Gregory had a team of specialists create a new, correct calendar.

La Pietà

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This statue is one of the most famous in the world. It is la Pietà by Michelangelo. Pietà means pity or sorrow. Michelangelo was only 23 years old and it took him one year to sculpt. It is the only artwork by the artist that is signed. If you look at the ribbon across Maria’s chest you can see his name. It says Michelangelo Buanorotte Florentino ha fatto questo, Michelangelo Buonarotti from Florence has made this. He signed this work, because one day he was looking at his statue and some people came in and said the statue was made by a different sculptor. Michelangelo got angry and chiseled his name in at night.

Michelangelo has been criticized for this work as well. Critics say that if Maria would stand up, she would be twice as big as Christ. Also, some considered Maria to be way too youthful. She looks younger than her son. The reason Maria looks big, is because nowadays the statue stands in the wrong position. Michelangelo created it thinking it would stand on ground level. So you would look at the statue from above. If you look at the statue like that, suddenly the perspective works. The same applies to the David in Florence. People criticize this statue because its hands, feet and head are too big for its body. This is because the statue was meant to stand on the Cathedral, far up. People would only be able to see it from far, so he made the hands and feet bigger so you can notice them.

Portico of St. Peter’s Basilica

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you enter under the balcony you will first end up in a hall. Here, on the far left and right sides you can see two statues. On the left side you can see a statue of Charlemagne, who was crowned by the Pope in the church in the year 800. On the right side is a more beautiful statue, made by Bernini. It is Constantine, the first Christian Emperor and the founder of the first Saint Peter’s Basilica. Here we see the vision of Constantine. He is sitting on his horse and looking up. In the sky he sees the sign of the cross. The next day we would make his soldiers paint this sign on their shields and they would go on to win the battle of the Milvian Bridge which would make Constantine Emperor. After this he started to sponsor Christianity. The main doors in the middle come from the old basilica. Inside there are 44 altars, 11 domes, 778 columns, 135 mosaic paintings and 395 statues. The church is 192 meters long. It is the largest church in the world and they have markings to prove it. In the middle of the church the names and lengths of other churches can be seen. This is why the middle section is blocked, so that people stop walking over the markings.

Ponte Sant’Angelo

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Ponte Sant’Angelo is the old bridge that connects the Vatican with the city Centre. It was built in 136 AD in the time of Emperor Hadrian. The three arches in the middle are the original ones from antiquity. On the bridge you can see 10 statues of angels. They were made by the workshop of Gian Lorenzo Bernini, the amazing baroque sculptor. Bernini made two angels himself, but the Pope considered them too beautiful to be put outside. All the designs are Bernini’s although his students executed the work. They all hold objects from the story of the passion of Christ. You can see their robes wave in the wind. This is because Bernini knew they would stand outside in the wind. Before, this was the only bridge to the Vatican and all the pilgrims had to cross this bridge. Now a new bridge is standing next to it on which cars can pass the river.

Castel Sant’Angelo

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Castel Sant’Angelo is a very strange building. Once it was the mausoleum for Emperor Hadrian. It was built in 135 AD. Later, Emperors Marcus Aurelius, Septimus Severus and Caracalla were also buried here.

In the middle ages it was transformed into a fortress, to protect the Vatican. It is called the fortress of angels because Pope Gregory the Great saw an angel appear above the building in 590. At that time Rome was suffering from an epidemic of plague. The Pope held 40 processions to stop the plague and at the end he saw Archangel Michael appear above the castle, who put his sword back in its holder. Everybody understood this meant the plague was over and the next day it was. Ever since the building is called Castel Sant’Angelo and a statue of Archangel Michael putting his sword in his holder stands on top of the building. The current statue dates from the 18th century.

The fortress is 5 stories high. Originally it must have looked quite different. Trees grew around a big statue of Hadrian in his chariot. Because the mausoleum was built on a very strategic spot, close to the only bridge to the Vatican, it was transformed in a fortress. It has been a papal residence and prison and five Popes were executed inside. Now it is a museum. From the top of the building you have a very beautiful view of Rome.

The Vatican Museums

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Vatican museums are 14 kilometers long and there are about 2000 rooms in the museum. It is said that if you spend 60 seconds looking at every item in the museum you will spend 12 years in here.

It is important to realize that the spaces you will walk through were never built as a museum. They were the private quarters of the Pope. So the museum is one direction only, just because some passageways are so small.

The founder of the museum was one of the most influential Pope’s. His name was Julius II. In 1506 a statue was found on Esquiline hill. Julius sent Michelangelo to examine it and on his recommendation he immediately bought it. He put it on display in the Vatican for all to see. The statue is still there and you will see it later. It is still one of the highlights of the museum.

The Balcony of Saint Peter’s Basilica

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The balcony of Saint Peter’s Basilica is where the Pope is presented to the people of the world after the conclave. It is the one above the main door, with the red color on the walls on both sides. When a new Pope is elected the gathering of cardinals is called a conclave. Conclave comes from the latin Cum Clave, which means with key. The cardinals are locked in the Sistine Chapel together, when they choose a new Pope from their midst. After a new Pope is elected, he will be presented to the people on this main balcony on Saint Peter’s.

It is also from this balcony the Pope blesses the city and the world every Easter, the Urbi et Orbi. Under the balcony there is a carving which shows Jesus handing the keys to heaven to Saint Peter.

The Statues of St. Peter and St. Paul

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

In front of the Saint Peter’s Basilica you can see two statues. They are St. Peter and St. Paul. You can recognize St. Peter by the keys in his hand, because he possesses the keys to heaven. St. Paul is always recognizable by the sword. This is because he was not crucified, but beheaded by a sword. This was his right as a roman citizen. Peter and Jesus were not roman citizens so they were crucified, which is a much more painful death. Both Peter and Paul are buried in Rome. Peter under Saint Peter’s and Paul is buried under San Paolo Fuori le Mura. The exact location of Saint Paul’s body was very recently discovered close to the altar in Saint Paul’s.

The Apostolic Palace

April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Papal Palaces you can see on your right when you stand on St. Peter’s square when you face the church, were built in the late 1500s. Inside, there are 1400 rooms, 20 courtyards, an Olympic sized swimming pool, a bowling alley and a movie theater. The Pope lives on the third floor and his window is on the top level. It is the 2nd window to the far right. From here the Pope does the blessing on Sundays. Pope Francis has decided not to live in the papal palaces. He prefers a more modest home in the very Spartan hotel Pope John Paul II had constructed to house the cardinals when they come for the conclave, the election of a new Pope.

The Obelisk on St. Peter’s Square

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The obelisk on St. Peter’s Square is 25 meters high, made from red granite. Emperor Caligula ordered to be erected around here. It came from Egypt, where it was created under the pharaoh’s. The Romans really liked all things Egyptian. We have to realise that the Egyptian culture was already 2000 years old in the time of Julius Caesar. People like him looked to the ancient Egyptians like we look to the Romans now. The Romans loved the obelisks of Egypt so much, that many of them were taken from Egypt to Rome. Now, there are more obelisks in Rome than in the whole of Egypt.

A special ship was made to transport this one from Egypt to Rome. On the pedestal it says Caligula dedicated it to his predecessors Augustus and Tiberius. It was placed on the square in 1586 during the Papacy of Sixtus V, who was obsessed by obelisks. He had them excavated all over town and resurrected. He had workers go around the city with long metal sticks to poke the earth around places where it was suspected obelisks could be found.

When this one was raised, none of the workers was allowed to speak, because they all had to be able to hear the instructions. The punishment for speaking was death, but when the obelisk was halfway up a sailor screamed ‘put water on the ropes’. He was not punished but rewarded because if this had not happened the ropes would have snapped and the obelisk would have fallen down.

Via della Conciliazione

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The main road to the Vatican is called the Via della Conciliazione. It means Reconciliation Street. It was built between 1936 and 1950. Before, there was a block of houses here and lots of small streets. You had to make your way through these small alleyways and then suddenly you would arrive at Saint Peter’s square. When Gian Lorenzo Bernini designed the square, that was exactly the surprise effect he was looking for. Many years later this surprise arrival at the square was made undone by Benito Mussolini, the fascist Italian dictator. He decided Saint Peter’s needed a big access road, which became the Via della Conciliazione. The name refers to the reconciliation between the church and the Italian state. The country Italy was only created in 1870. Before, the Popes ruled the area around Rome, which was known as the Papal States. When the Italian republicans conquered the city and made it the new capital of a unified Italy, the Pope locked himself angrily away in Vatican City. No official borders between the two states were drawn until 1929, when Mussolini and the Pope came to an understanding of what the borders of the Vatican would be. The resulting treaty is called the Lateran treaty of 1929. In this treaty the exact boundaries of Vatican City were drawn. The Pope decided he wanted the Vatican to be as small as possible, to make sure the church would not be drawn into worldly affairs anymore.

To build this street, many old houses and churches were torn down. On both sides of the street you can see many obelisks. The Romans call these jokingly suppositories.

The Vatican

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you walked down Via della Conciliazione towards Saint Peter’s, you enter a different country once you cross the street and stand on Saint Peter’s square. So first of, let’s talk a bit about how this country came to be. When the Western Roman Empire fell in 476 AD Italy entered difficult times. Many different barbarians invaded the peninsula, like the Goths, the Vandals and the Huns. There was no more Emperor in Rome. So the Bishop of Rome, the Pope, became the ruler of the city and in the next few centuries the power of the papacy grew very fast. Many rich people left their land to the papacy after they died, to secure a place in heaven, and slowly the Pope became the ruler of most of the land around Rome. This was the beginning of the Papal States. The Papal States were comprised of what are now the Umbria and Lazio regions. These lands remained in the possession of the Pope until 1870 when Italy was unified. Rome was declared the capital of the new Italian kingdom. The Pope at that time, Pius IX, was really unhappy about losing all his land and so he locked himself up inside the Vatican City and never left again.
