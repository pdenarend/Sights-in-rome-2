St. Paul outside the Walls

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The St. Paul outside the Walls or San Paolo fuori le Mura, is one of the four Papal basilicas in Rome. It was founded by Emperor Constantine to mark the grave of the apostle. It was expanded and enlarged in the following centuries, until it became even bigger than the old Saint Peter’s. The current Saint Peter’s Basilica of course is bigger. It is called outside the walls, because the grave of the apostle was outside of the city walls of Rome. Saint Paul was martyred using a sword. This was his right, because he was a Roman citizen. Jesus and Peter were not so they were crucified. Paul is almost always depicted with a sword in his hands.

Santa Maria in Trastevere

April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Santa Maria in Trastevere is the first church that was dedicated to Maria and according to locals the first church where Christians held public mass. The first Christians came from the Jewish community, who lived in Trastevere. The current church dates from 1140 and was built with stones from the baths of Caracalla. The mosaic on the facade is from also from that time. We see Mary on a throne surrounded by five female figures that hold small lamps. A burning flame means they are virgins, if there is no flame they are widows. Mary gives milk to baby Jesus using a small milk bottle. A bottle was used because people thought a naked breast would not be proper to put on the facade of a church.

Portico of St. Peter’s Basilica

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you enter under the balcony you will first end up in a hall. Here, on the far left and right sides you can see two statues. On the left side you can see a statue of Charlemagne, who was crowned by the Pope in the church in the year 800. On the right side is a more beautiful statue, made by Bernini. It is Constantine, the first Christian Emperor and the founder of the first Saint Peter’s Basilica. Here we see the vision of Constantine. He is sitting on his horse and looking up. In the sky he sees the sign of the cross. The next day we would make his soldiers paint this sign on their shields and they would go on to win the battle of the Milvian Bridge which would make Constantine Emperor. After this he started to sponsor Christianity. The main doors in the middle come from the old basilica. Inside there are 44 altars, 11 domes, 778 columns, 135 mosaic paintings and 395 statues. The church is 192 meters long. It is the largest church in the world and they have markings to prove it. In the middle of the church the names and lengths of other churches can be seen. This is why the middle section is blocked, so that people stop walking over the markings.

St. Peter’s Basilica

April 10, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Welcome to the heart of Catholicism, Saint Peter’s Basilica. If you enter the church for the first time you might need some time to get accustomed to the sheer size of the church and its decorations. Don’t worry, it is normal. Just move away from the masses and let it sink in for a bit.

Let’s talk a bit about the history of this site. Do you know what this site was before St. Peter’s Basilica was built? It was Caligula’s Circus, which was later enlarged by Nero. Nero reigned from 54AD to 68AD and during his reign there was a great fire in 64AD. Nero had to blame somebody for the fire, because some people said he started it himself to make room for his new Golden Palace, the Domus Aurea. So Nero blamed the Christians, which was considered a new strange sect and told them that the Roman Gods were angry at them for worshiping only one God.

San Clemente

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The San Clemente is one of the most interesting churches in Rome. It consists of three layers. Under the main church from the 12th century you can find another church from the 4th century and under that there is an old roman house with a sanctuary to Mithras. The first church on the middle level was erected in the 4th century and named after the 4th Pope Saint Clement, who used to live on this spot. This church was partly destroyed in the 11th century by a Norman conqueror and then a new church was built on top of the remains of the others.

Clement was banished to the Crimea by the Romans because of his success in converting people to Christianity, but even from there he kept on converting lots of people. He then was given an ultimatum: Or worship the Roman gods or die. He decided to continue converting and was then thrown into the Black sea, tied to an anchor. Afterwards, the sea would open once a year, so people could erect a shrine and worship Clements relics. On one of these occasions a widow lost her only son, but he was found alive and well a year later when the sea opened again.

Santi Quattro Coronati

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santi Quattro Coronati is a very interesting but relatively unknown church. It used to be a fortress and monastery, which protected the Lateran palace, where the Popes used to live before they moved to southern France and then to the Vatican. It was built in the 4th century and rebuilt in the 11th century after the Normans attacked. Now, Augustinian nuns live here. The floor is especially notable, because at least 200 antique marble stones were used to make the pattern.

The Baptistery of St. John Lateran

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you have time try to go and see the Baptistery. It is a small building just outside St. John Lateran. It is the oldest of Rome and according to legend this is the place Emperor Constantine would have been baptized by Pope Sylvester I. The current building dates from 432. Its octagonal shape would influence the building of all later Baptisteries all over the world. The octagonal shape represents the 8th day of creation, when a man is created again by being baptized. It is built on the baths of the old house of Faustina, the second wife of Constantine. The basin is an old roman bathtub made from green basalt. The basin had to be big, because in this time adults were converted to Christianity and had to be baptized by submerging their whole body in water.

You can see frescoes with scenes from the life of John the Baptist and also scenes from the life of Constantine. The mosaic is from the 5th century. The deer represents the renewal of life. The deer blows water towards snakes in holes in the earth to get them out and trample them, just like god uses water to baptize people and drive out evil. The deer always looks for water to drink which represents humanities need for baptism.

Santa Maria della Vittoria

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria della Vittoria is a very small church. Its fame derives from the statue which can be found inside. It is one of Bernini’s masterpieces, Teresa in Ecstasy. The church used to be a convent of the Carmelites. This order was reformed by Saint Teresa of Ávila. Teresa is one of three female doctors of the Church. She died in the night of the 4th to the 15th of October 1582. Incidentally, this was the night when a few days were skipped because of the introduction of the Gregorian calendar. She was canonized in 1622 and a few decades later Bernini designed the Cornaro chapel in this church to honor her.

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria degli Angeli e dei Martiri is a church, built inside the ruins of the Baths of Diocletian. These baths were built in 305 AD and could hold 3000 people. The small middle part was transformed by Michelangelo into a church. If you have the chance, go inside, because it is amazing. You can see the simple doors and facade on one side of the square. Michelangelo decided against the building of a facade. He wanted to leave the ruins as they are. But when you go inside you can see how big the building is.

Santa Prassede

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This church is called Santa Maria Prassede, and although it is not well known, it is one of the oldest and most beautiful churches of Rome. The church is dedicated Praxedes, the daughter of a Roman senator, Pudens. Peter stayed in his house for some time and converted him to Christianity, together with her daughters Praxedes and Pudentiana. Praxedes dedicated her life to helping the poor and persecuted. She hid Christians in her house to protect them from Roman persecutions. When the Romans found out where their hiding place was they killed all 23 of them. Praxedes then used a sponge to collect their blood, which she kept in the well of her house. The Church is built on the location of her house.

Santa Maria Maggiore

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria Maggiore is one of the four papal basilicas of Rome and is also Vatican territory. It was built on the top of Esquiline hill, because of a dream of a Pope. In his dream, Pope Liberius was visited by an angel, who told him to build a church on top of the hill where snow would fall the next day. Of course, it was the middle of August, and snow does not normally fall at that time of the year. But the Pope woke up and saw that snow had fallen on top of Esquiline hill. He went over and discovered that the owner of the land had had the same dream. They then decided to build the church. This was in the 4th century. Every august this event is remembered by throwing white flowers in the air on August the 5th. The campanile, the bell tower, is a Romanesque structure from the 14th century. The facade is both baroque and medieval. If you stand in front of the Church, you can see that a newer, baroque facade is built around the older one. You can still see the medieval mosaic on what used to be the facade.

On the square in front of the church stands a Corinthian column, which came from Constantine’s Basilica on the Forum. On top, a bronze statue of Mary was put in 1614.

The Church of St. Bartholomew

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The church on the island is dedicated to St. Bartholomew, one of the apostles of Jesus. It was built around the year 1000 and a few centuries later the remains of the apostle Bartholomew were brought to the church, after which it was dedicated to him. His remains are buried below the main altar and the plate on which the remains were brought here can be seen on the right next to the altar. Michelangelo painted Bartholomew in his fresco of the last judgment in the Sistine Chapel. He is the one holding his own skin in his hand, because he was skinned alive.

San Luigi dei Francesi

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The San Luigi dei Francesi is the national church of France in Rome. It was designed by Giacomo della Porta and executed by Domenico Fontana between 1518 and 1589. The facade has several statues with famous people from the history of France, like Charlemagne, Saint Louis, Saint Clothilde and Saint Jeanne of Valois.

Inside is one of the masterpieces of Domenichino, a famous baroque painter. On the ceiling he painted the histories of Saint Cecilia. Domenichino was for a period considered one of the best painters of all time, but his fame is somewhat diminished now.

Sant’Ignazio

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you pass by the Sant’Ignazio, you should definitely go in, even if it is just for a second. But before you do so, have a look at the Piazza Sant’Ignazio in front of it. It is a very nice example of baroque architecture. Look at how playful the buildings are built. This is not a geometric design of the renaissance anymore. Everything is rounded. Then, when you go inside, you will be amazed by the ceiling of the church.

It is an amazing example of perspective painting. If you stand on the marker in the middle of the nave of the church and look up, the perspective in the ceiling fresco is exactly right and the ceiling looks much higher than it actually is. But if you move to other parts of the church, the illusion is broken. You can try it by walking all the way to the altar and look back to the ceiling of the nave. It is an amazing fresco and it was painted by a Jesuit monk, called Andrea Pozzo, who was also a mathematician. In it, he depicts the triumph of Saint Ignatius, the founder of the Jesuit order. Right in the middle of the fresco you can see Jesus, hanging on to his cross. From Jesus, a ray of light shoots towards Ignatius, who is sitting on a cloud. The light is then reflected by Ignatius and illuminates the four continents. These continents are reflected by the four pillars in the corners of the fresco. Their names are written on the pillars as well: Africa, Europe, Asia and America. Australia had not yet been discovered. The continents are also recognizable by symbols. A Native American can be seen around the American pillar and a black queen around the African.

Then there is also the dome. The dome is not real, but also painted. The monks of the adjacent monastery did not want the big dome blocking the light in their library, so they asked the Jesuits to not build a dome. They did not build a dome, but instead asked Andrea Pozzo to paint one. It is a gigantic trompe-l’oeil.

The church itself is a typical example of Roman baroque style. It is, of course, a Jesuit church and the baroque was a style the Jesuits often liked to use in their buildings.

Santa Maria Sopra Minerva

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

This church, adjacent to the Pantheon, is the only Gothic church in Rome. It is called Santa Maria Sopra Minerva. Sopra means above, so the church was built on top of a temple dedicated to Minerva. Churches were often built on top of temples. After Christianity replaced paganism, the temples were no longer needed and were considered blasphemous. So they were torn down and with the building material recovered churches were built.

The facade is simple and dates from the renaissance, but the interior of the church is gothic. The gothic style was never very popular in Italy and at the time of the style’s popularity the Popes lived in Avignon in France, so not much was built in Rome at that time. The church belongs to the Dominican friars. On the right side on the facade markers can be seen which show how high the water would rise sometimes. This area was prone to flooding until the banks of the Tiber were strengthened in the 19th century.

The Pantheon

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Pantheon is one of the most impressive monuments of ancient Rome. ‘Whoever visits Rome without visiting the Pantheon, returns like an ass’ is what the Romans say. This is probably the best preserved monument from antiquity. According to legend it is built on the spot where Romulus, founder of Rome, was taken by a cloud during a thunderstorm and forever disappeared. On the frieze you can see the words M.AGRIPPA.L.F.COS. TERTIUM.FECIT which means Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius built this during his third consulate. These letters are not original, but were added later. Thanks to the holes in the frieze historians found out what the original letters said and then replicas were made. For a long time people thought the Pantheon was built in the time of Emperor Augustus. Not a strange thought, considering on the facade it says so.

Santa Maria dell’Anima

April 8, 2015April 8, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santa Maria dell’Anima is the German church. You can find it just behind Piazza Navona. Around this church the German pilgrims used to stay when they came to Rome. In the church is also the grave of the last non-Italian Pope before John Paul II. He was Hadrian VI and he was Dutch. He was made Pope because of the influence of his employer and student, Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor. His pontificate was not a success. He was way too strict for the Romans to handle. They were used to Popes like Alexander VI and Julius II, who had children and were more like renaissance princes. Everybody was very relieved when Hadrian died very soon. Signs of gratitude were put at the door of the Popes physician, who had not been able (or wanting) to save him.

History of St. Peter’s Basilica

April 8, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Do you know what this site was before St. Peter’s Basilica was built?  It was the Roman Emperor Caligula’s Circus, which was later enlarged by Nero. Nero reigned from 54AD to 68AD and during his reign there was a great fire in 64AD. A big part of Rome was turned into ashes. Fires were an enormous problem in that time. Fire departments did not exist. If a fire broke out, Romans would just tear down the buildings around the fire, to make sure it would not spread. Nero had to blame somebody for the fire, because some people said he started it himself to make room for his new Golden Palace, the Domus Aurea. So Nero blamed the Christians, which was considered a new strange sect and told them that the Roman Gods were angry at them for worshiping only one God.
