Around St. John Lateran

San Clemente

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The San Clemente is one of the most interesting churches in Rome. It consists of three layers. Under the main church from the 12th century you can find another church from the 4th century and under that there is an old roman house with a sanctuary to Mithras. The first church on the middle level was erected in the 4th century and named after the 4th Pope Saint Clement, who used to live on this spot. This church was partly destroyed in the 11th century by a Norman conqueror and then a new church was built on top of the remains of the others.

Clement was banished to the Crimea by the Romans because of his success in converting people to Christianity, but even from there he kept on converting lots of people. He then was given an ultimatum: Or worship the Roman gods or die. He decided to continue converting and was then thrown into the Black sea, tied to an anchor. Afterwards, the sea would open once a year, so people could erect a shrine and worship Clements relics. On one of these occasions a widow lost her only son, but he was found alive and well a year later when the sea opened again.

Santi Quattro Coronati

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Santi Quattro Coronati is a very interesting but relatively unknown church. It used to be a fortress and monastery, which protected the Lateran palace, where the Popes used to live before they moved to southern France and then to the Vatican. It was built in the 4th century and rebuilt in the 11th century after the Normans attacked. Now, Augustinian nuns live here. The floor is especially notable, because at least 200 antique marble stones were used to make the pattern.

Scala Sancta

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

Next to Saint John in Lateran, on the other side of the street, you can find the Scala Sancta. This building houses the holy steps. The building was built by Domenico Fontana in 1589. According to legend Christ climbed the steps in this building on the Friday of his death, going to Pontius Pilatus’ house. The mother of Emperor Constantine, Saint Helen, would have taken the steps from Pilatus’ house in Jerusalem in 326 AD. This story only starts being told in the 7th century though. Only faithful are allowed to ascend the steps, and only on their knees. The steps are protected by wood and on some places you can see some blood, allegedly from Christ.

The Baptistery of St. John Lateran

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

If you have time try to go and see the Baptistery. It is a small building just outside St. John Lateran. It is the oldest of Rome and according to legend this is the place Emperor Constantine would have been baptized by Pope Sylvester I. The current building dates from 432. Its octagonal shape would influence the building of all later Baptisteries all over the world. The octagonal shape represents the 8th day of creation, when a man is created again by being baptized. It is built on the baths of the old house of Faustina, the second wife of Constantine. The basin is an old roman bathtub made from green basalt. The basin had to be big, because in this time adults were converted to Christianity and had to be baptized by submerging their whole body in water.

You can see frescoes with scenes from the life of John the Baptist and also scenes from the life of Constantine. The mosaic is from the 5th century. The deer represents the renewal of life. The deer blows water towards snakes in holes in the earth to get them out and trample them, just like god uses water to baptize people and drive out evil. The deer always looks for water to drink which represents humanities need for baptism.

The Obelisk of St. John Lateran

April 9, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The Egyptian Obelisk which stands on the piazza on the side of St. John Lateran is the oldest and highest in Rome. It is 3500 years old and 31 meters high. It was taken from Thebes to Rome in 357 BC in a boat. It used to stand at the Circus Maximus and was later put here by the Popes.

St. John Lateran

April 9, 2015April 10, 2015 Paul den Arend Leave a comment

The St. John Lateran is one of the most important churches in Rome. The area on which it is built once belonged to the Lateran family. Their property was confiscated because they had conspired against Emperor Nero and had sheltered Christians in their home. In the fourth century AD it was given by Emperor Constantine to the Pope, who built a church there. The Popes lived here also until 1305.
